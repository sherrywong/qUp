    Location-based social networks

    ABSTRACT

    Location-based social networks (LBSNs) provide a rich paradigm for semantically rich quantitative representation of urban dynamics in terms of mobility flows, communication and social interaction patterns. This talk illustrates several applications of using social media and telecommunication data to discover patterns of urban dynamics and detect events in cities within an LBSN framework. It also outlines possible approaches to design recommender systems and decision support tools for novel bottom-up control policies.

    Emotion Tracking and Interventions for Memory, Health and Awareness

    ABSTRACT

    In this talk I will describe novel systems and applications we are designing that perform mood detection and interventions in real time using mobile technology. We are exploring “sticky” user interface ideas to help users reflect on and manage their affective experiences. Many questions remain from current affective computing research in terms of how useful technology like this is over the long term and how valuable a mobile tracking system might be in real time (especially given the likelihood of misclassifications). In addition, we also are interested in intervention styles that can be used when negative or disruptive emotions are detected, whether in a car, at the desktop, or otherwise. Finally, we feel there is a huge opportunity in the remote familial space, or in a close social network, where knowing about the emotional health of separated loved ones or close friends comes in to play. These new research areas are tightly coupled to privacy issues. A few examples of applications from our research will be presented.

    Measuring and Reducing Individuals' Carbon Footprints

    ABSTRACT

    American households are responsible for 40 to 98 percent of total U.S. GHG emissions, depending on modeling choices. Moreover, 7 to 20 percent of household GHG emissions can be eliminated through behavior changes that result in net cost savings. And, given sufficient information and incentives, some households may also be willing to completely offset their emissions. Oroeco, tracks individuals' emissions incurred by their purchasing behavior. I'll discuss our approach briefly, with the intent to stimulate discussion surrounding tradeoffs between allocating limited engineering resources between improving measurement accuracy vs. incentives or other change motivators.

    Behavior, Routines and Narrative

    This lecture will review a few important but easily-forgotten attributes of people: We are a social species, and we depend on language to develop as a culture, i.e. to go beyond hunting and gathering. These two principles shape how we develop and change, and in particular how our behavior develops and changes. This talk is an attempt at a synthesis of the role of narrative in behavior change - how the language of others influences what we do, and how the internalization of language shapes who we are and who we aspire to be. For the first question we have been studying the efficacy of "dialogic" messages, especially when presented on mobile phones. We find that a dialog-like presentation is more effective than a didactic one with the same message content. For the second question we are studying models of "identity-as-language", i.e. how humans self-model through the language that they use to talk about themselves, and how these models predict their resilience to adversity and their efficacy at self-change. Finally, the identity-as-narrative model can provide a handle on some of the trickiest questions about behavior - "what is it that people really want to do?". We will conclude with an exercise.

    Behavior Statics to Behavioral Dynamics? The ITS Context

    The field of Intelligent Transportation Systems, or ITS, is an engagement between information technology and transportation. ITS has 30 years of impact on the supply side through advances in ramp metering, corridor signal control, bus scheduling, and other such genres. These have graduated from sensing and monitoring to feedback control. However, the benefits of a transportation investment depend not only on the production function of the investment, but also the use citizens make of it, i.e., citizen choice or demand. Programmers informing citizen behavior such as parking pricing, cordon pricing, Spare the Air Day, or Bike to work day, are much less engaged with monitoring or control as in dynamical systems. I explore why and whether we should move from Behavioral Statics to Behavioral Dynamics through the lens of our work on Quantified Traveler and XMobile.

    Understanding Behavior Requires Understanding the Preferences that Lie Behind It.

    Risk and time preferences are important inputs into any broader measure of social welfare and enter virtually every realm of individual decision-making. Understanding risk and time preferences thus has broad-reaching implications in many disciplines, ranging from economics, to psychology, to public health. The use of economic theory in experimental designs and empirical analyses provides a richer understanding risk and time preferences and the choices that implement them. The research is motivated by five types of questions fundamental to empirical analysis:

    1. Consistency. Is behavior consistent with a model of utility maximization?
    2. Structure. Is the observed data consistent with a utility function with some special structural properties?
    3. Recoverability. Can underlying preferences be recovered from observed choices?
    4. Correlates. Can heterogeneity in choice be explained, with the aid of economic theory, in terms of demographic and economic variables?
    5. Extrapolation. Can choices in natural decision environments be predicted with behaviors in the laboratory?

    The work has made substantive contributions to the study of individual heterogeneity and bounded rationality and has also contributed to experimental methodology. The entire apparatus --- theory, experimental methodology, and econometrics for linking theory to data --- has provided useful tools for a range of disciplines interested in individual choice.

    Challenges in Implementing Behavioral Change Field Experiments: An Example from an RCT Incentivizing Safer Sex

    This lecture will discuss a randomized field experiment that tested the use of incentives for reducing risky sexual behaviors. We will discuss the challenges inherent in fielding such trials and in measuring behavioral change.
